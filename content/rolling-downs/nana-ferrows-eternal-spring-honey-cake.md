I was not going to write this post today.

I had a whole different article planned. A roundup of the best spring jams at the Millhaven market (there are so many good ones this year, you have no idea), but then I woke up this morning and the light was doing that thing it does, that golden thing where it comes through the kitchen window at just the right angle and lands on the counter in this one perfect stripe, and I thought: *today is a honey cake day.*

And then I thought: *every day is a honey cake day, if you let it be.*

And then I started crying a little, which is fine, because that is what spring does to me, and it has been spring for twenty years.

---

Let me tell you about my Nana.

Nana Ferrow lived on the southern end of the Rolling Downs, in a stone house with green shutters and a door that never closed right. She had a garden that could feed a village and a laugh that could wake one up. She was not a fancy woman. She did not read the Chronicle (sorry, editors). She did not care about politics or university research or what the Evernal had to say about anything. She cared about feeding people. That was it. That was the whole thing.

Every spring, when the cherry blossoms opened along the Fenwater, Nana would make her honey cake. She made it with wildflower honey from a man named Dorran who kept bees on the ridge above Brannock Crossing. She made it with butter she churned herself because she said store butter "tasted like giving up." She made it with three eggs, always three, because she said two was lazy and four was showing off.

She made it in a cast iron pan that was older than anyone in the family could account for.

I did not appreciate this cake when I was young. I want to be honest about that. I thought it was too simple. I wanted chocolate. I wanted frosting. I wanted the things you want when you are small and do not yet understand that simplicity is not the absence of effort but the result of so much effort that the effort disappears.

I understand now.

---

The thing about spring in the Rolling Downs is that it never stops being beautiful. I know that sounds like a complaint dressed as a compliment, and maybe it is, a little. When the Silence came, everything froze in place. The blossoms stayed. The light stayed. The mornings kept arriving in that same golden way, over and over, and at first it was lovely and then it was unsettling and now it is just... what it is.

But the honey cake still works.

I do not know why. Everything else has gotten strange. The cherry trees bloom and bloom and never fruit. The bees still make honey, somehow, and nobody at the university can explain that either (they cannot explain most things, in my experience, but they are very confident about it). The flour comes in from outside the region now because our wheat does not mature past the green stage. But the cake still comes together. The batter still turns that perfect shade of amber. The kitchen still smells the way Nana's kitchen smelled.

My daughter, Posey, sits on the counter and watches me make it, the same way I watched Nana. She is six. She will not remember a time before the Silence. She will grow up thinking spring is all there is. But she will know this cake, and she will know that someone made it for someone who made it for someone, and that the recipe survived, even if the seasons did not.

I am crying again. That is fine.

---

My husband, Aldworth, says I am "a lot" about this cake. He is correct. He married me anyway. He eats two slices every time and says "this is good, Clem" in that quiet way he has, and I know he means it because Aldworth does not say things he does not mean. He is from the northern part of the Downs where people do not waste words on feelings. He shows love by eating seconds. That is enough for me.

---

Anyway. You came here for the recipe.

I know you did. I know some of you scrolled past everything I just wrote. That is fine. I will not pretend it does not sting a little, but I understand. You are busy. It is spring and you have things to do (the same things, probably, because it is always spring, but still).

Here it is. Nana Ferrow's Eternal Spring Honey Cake. Please make it with love, or at least with decent butter.

---

## Nana Ferrow's Eternal Spring Honey Cake

**Prep time:** 15 minutes
**Bake time:** 35 to 40 minutes
**Serves:** 8, or 4 if nobody is pretending to be polite

### Ingredients

- 2 cups all-purpose flour (imported is fine, do what you can)
- 1 and 1/2 teaspoons baking powder
- 1/2 teaspoon salt
- 1/2 teaspoon cinnamon
- 1/4 teaspoon ground cardamom (Nana did not use this, I added it, she would forgive me)
- 1/2 cup butter, softened (real butter, do not test me)
- 3/4 cup wildflower honey (Dorran's if you can get it, any local honey if you cannot)
- 3 eggs (three, exactly three)
- 1 teaspoon vanilla
- 1/2 cup buttermilk
- Zest of one lemon, if the lemons are cooperating

### Directions

1. Heat your oven to 350 degrees. Grease a 9-inch cast iron pan or cake tin. If you do not own cast iron, I am not angry, I am just disappointed.

2. Whisk the flour, baking powder, salt, cinnamon, and cardamom together in a bowl. Set it aside.

3. In a larger bowl, beat the butter and honey together until they are smooth and light. This will take longer than you think. Keep going.

4. Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each. Add the vanilla.

5. Alternate adding the flour mixture and the buttermilk, starting and ending with the flour. Mix until just combined. Do not overwork it. Nana said overworked batter makes a cake that tastes like it is trying too hard, and she was right about everything.

6. Fold in the lemon zest if using.

7. Pour the batter into your prepared pan. Smooth the top.

8. Bake for 35 to 40 minutes, until the top is golden and a knife inserted in the center comes out clean.

9. Let it cool in the pan for at least 10 minutes. Drizzle with a little extra honey if you like. Serve warm.

---

That is it. That is the whole cake. No frosting, no layers, no decorations. Just honey and butter and flour and eggs and time.

Make it for someone you love. Or make it for yourself, because you are also someone worth feeding.

Happy spring. Again.
