I do not know how to write this one, and I have been writing for these pages a long time.

Posy Hartwell is gone. Most of you already know how, and I am not going to spend her memorial on the water or on the advisory or on any of it, because Posy would have hated that, and because she deserves a page that is about her life and not the last hour of it. She was forty-one. She leaves three children. She leaves the rest of us, too, though we do not get a line in the official notices, so I am taking one here.

You knew her voice whether you knew her name or not. If you have ever read a Downs roundup and thought, well, I suppose we could take the little ones to the fern meadows this weekend, that was Posy, reaching across the paper and into your kitchen and getting your family out the door. She wrote about first broom lessons over the meadows and the morning queue at Grandma Windward's Beastiary and which market stall would let a four-year-old pet the griffin chicks without losing a finger. She wrote a warning about pegasus foals that I still think about every time I pass a paddock. She once ended a column by asking us to note, for the record, that two of her three children were at that moment outside, and that was the whole of her philosophy right there. Get them out. Let them count the tadpoles. The world is worth showing them.

She was, in person, exactly the woman you would hope wrote those columns. Warm without being soft, funny in a dry way that snuck up on you, endlessly patient with everyone's children and slightly less patient with everyone's opinions about her own. We met years ago at the honey cake stall, both of us ostensibly volunteering and mostly eating, and she looked at me and said, "You are the cake woman, and I am the family woman, so between us we have covered the entire reason anyone reads the Downs pages." We were friends from that morning on.

I keep thinking about how much of what she wrote was about safety. Bring a change of clothes, she would say. Bring two. Keep one eye on the water. Know where the shallow bit ends. She spent years teaching this region's parents how to let their children be children without coming to harm, and there is a cruelty in how she left us that I cannot make sit right and am not going to pretend to. So I will not try. I will only say that nothing about it was her failing. She did everything she ever told the rest of us to do.

What I want to leave you with is not the grief, which is ours to carry, but the instruction, which was always hers to give. She would want the children out this weekend. Not at the lake, not now, but out. In the meadows, at the market, up where the light does that golden thing through the trees. She believed, all the way down, that the small ordinary outings were the point of a life, and that you did not get them back once the season turned. She was right about that the way she was right about most things.

Posy is survived by her three children, whom this whole community now holds. If you are able to help the family in the days ahead, the Downs mutual aid table at Freeman's is collecting, and I will be there most mornings, ostensibly volunteering.

Go outside this weekend. Take someone small with you. That is the only tribute she ever asked for, and she asked for it in every single column she wrote.

*Clementine Ferrow-Aldworth writes about food, family, and life in the Rolling Downs. She was proud to call Posy Hartwell her friend.*
