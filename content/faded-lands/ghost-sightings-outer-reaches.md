The Board went back and forth on whether to print this, and decided we are now getting the reports faster than we can lose them. What began as the odd uneasy tale traded at the well has become, this last fortnight, something the strip talks about every morning. People out here are seeing the dead. Not all of them believe it. Nearly all of them have seen it.

Three accounts keep coming. The first is the hollow houses, the dozen empty homes down the strip whose families the fires took or the grange housing drew off. They are supposed to be dark, and since the turn of the month they have not all been. Families on the second creek keep reporting lamplight behind the shutters of the old place at the end of the lane, low and warm and gone by the time anyone reaches the door, with no ash in the grate and no boot in the mud.

The second is the woman on the Downs road, the most reported of all. At dusk, out past the waystation, she walks in a grey shawl with something held against her hip the way you carry a basket or a child. She keeps pace with your cart, does not answer a greeting, and is not there when you turn to offer her a ride. It is the courtesy meeting empty air that undoes people.

The third is the pale woman at the crossing, and the children are the ones who see her. Several who cut across the second creek on their way home describe a still, white-faced woman at that blue hour before full dark, watching the road as though waiting on someone who is late. She has not spoken and has not moved toward them. But they have all stopped taking that path, and children do not give up a shortcut for nothing.

The Board will not tell you what these are. The strip is split. Some say plainly that the dead are walking, that in a spring that will not turn the ones who should have moved on have found the door closed and come wandering back. Others, good Evernal folk, hold that naming a sorrow only feeds it, and that the faithful thing is to keep your eyes on your own door and let what passes pass in silence. We only observe that the silence does not appear to be working, and that the sightings have grown more frequent in exactly the weeks the strip has tried hardest to say nothing.

So we will say this much. Do not follow her off the road. Do not go into the lit house to be neighborly. Do not make your children take the crossing. And send us what you have seen. We will keep writing it down for as long as it keeps coming, which shows no sign of being a short while.
