The Board has gone back and forth on whether to print this, and the deciding argument was simply that we are now getting the reports faster than we can lose them. What began this spring as the odd uneasy tale traded at the well has become, in the last fortnight, something the strip talks about every morning. People out here are seeing the dead. Not all of them believe it. Nearly all of them have seen it. This notice is an attempt to set the accounts side by side, plainly, because a thing written down is a thing you can at least look at in daylight.

Here is what has come to the Board, sorted as best we can.

The hollow houses. There are a dozen empty houses scattered down the strip and along the far shore, homes whose families the fires took or the grange housing drew off, and one at least whose owner passed two winters ago and has no one to claim it. Those houses are supposed to be dark. Since the turn of the month they have not all been. Three separate families on the second creek have reported lamplight moving behind the shutters of the old place at the end of the lane, low and warm and gone by the time anyone reaches the door. No ash in the grate. No boot in the mud. Just the memory of a light, and the sense, every one of them said, of having interrupted something that had been going on quite comfortably without them.

The woman on the Downs road. This is the most reported of all, better than a dozen accounts now and from people who have never met. A woman walks the Downs road at dusk, out past the waystation, in a grey shawl, carrying something against her hip the way you carry a basket or a child. She keeps pace with your cart for a stretch. She does not answer a greeting. And she is not there when you turn your head to offer her a ride, which every decent soul on that road has tried to do, because that is how the strip was raised, and the not-being-there is the part that sends them to us shaken. You can abide a stranger. It is the courtesy meeting empty air that undoes people.

The pale man at the crossing. The children have a version too, and the Board debated leaving theirs out and decided that would be its own kind of lie. Several of the young ones who cut across the second creek on their way home have described a pale man who stands at the crossing at that blue hour before full dark, very still, very white in the face, watching the road as though waiting on someone who is late. He has not spoken to them. He has not moved toward them. But they have stopped taking that path, all of them, without any grown person telling them to, and children do not agree to give up a shortcut for nothing.

The Board is not going to tell you what these are. We are notice-takers, not priests, and the truth is the strip is split down the middle on the question. There are those who say plainly that the dead are walking, that in a spring that will not turn and a season that will not end, the ones who ought to have moved on have found the door closed and are wandering back to the only country they know. And there are those, good Evernal folk, who hold that naming a sorrow only feeds it, that the kind and faithful thing is to keep your eyes on your own door and let what passes pass in silence. The Board respects both houses. The Board only observes that the silence does not appear to be working, and that the sightings have grown more frequent, not less, in exactly the weeks the strip has tried hardest to say nothing.

So we will say something, which is this. If you see her on the road, do not follow her off it. If you see the light in the hollow house, do not go in to be neighborly. If your children will not take the crossing, do not make them. Whatever the outer reaches are seeing, it is coming more often and closer in, and this Board would rather you were kind to your neighbors while they are still the sort you can hand a warm plate to.

Send us what you have seen. We will keep writing it down for as long as it keeps coming, which at present shows no sign of being a short while.

*Hollis Barrow keeps the ledger and the odd tale for the Faded Lands Community Board. Sightings, and arguments about whether they ought to be printed, may be sent by owl.*
