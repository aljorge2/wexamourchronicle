The Board went around on this one the way we went around on the ghosts, and for the same reason. There are questions out here you are not supposed to ask aloud, and the oldest folk on the strip will tell you that asking is how you get an answer you did not want. But we have decided, again, that a thing everyone privately wonders is better set down on paper than left to fester at the well. So here it is, plainly. What is south of the town?

Consider what we actually know, which is almost nothing, and notice that the nothing points in one direction. Every road worth the name runs north. The Downs road goes north with its waystations and its lit windows. The strip itself is strung along the way to the gate, toward Vaelthir and the world that keeps its lamps burning. Even the pirate woman in the red coat, they say, asked after the road north. Nobody asks after the road south, because past a certain point there is no road to ask after. Beyond Garrick Thornby's thin acres, past the second creek, the ruts give out, the fence posts stop, and the last honest map of this region simply ends in pale paper. Somebody chose, a long time ago, not to draw it. That is the part that keeps the Board up.

We put the question to the strip this fortnight, and we got back four answers, which is to say we got back no agreement at all, which is itself worth reporting.

The first and most common answer is the flattest one: nothing. Empty moor and thin grass and then, eventually, the coast curving away toward Harrowmere, and between here and there not one reason to go. The people who say this say it firmly and quickly and then change the subject. The Board notes, without comment, that a person can be very sure about a place they will also tell you they have never been.

The second answer is the ruins. There are those on the strip, mostly the old ones, who will tell you there was a country down there before ours, older than the town, older than the founding, and that the roads run north because the people who made them were walking away from something and never once looked back over their shoulder. Standing stones, they say. Foundations in the heather where no family remembers a house. A well with good water and no farm around it. Whether any living soul has walked out to see these things, we could not confirm. The stories are always about someone's grandfather.

The third answer is the one the Board argued over whether to print, and printed anyway. Some folk, quietly, will tell you that south is not a place at all but a direction things go. They mean the season. They mean the ones who have gone pale and slow and then simply gone. There is a belief taking hold on the strip, no firmer than a belief and we will call it nothing more, that whatever has been thinning our people out this spring did not carry them off north toward the lit town but the other way, out past the second creek, toward the blank part of the map, because that is where a thing goes when it does not want to be followed. We report this because people are saying it, not because the Board credits it. But we will not pretend we have not heard it. We have heard little else.

The fourth answer is the oldest, and it is not really an answer. It is the Evernal one, and you get it from the devout and from a good many who are not: do not ask. Keep your life within sight of your door. Let the south be south. There is an old strip saying, older than the faith even, that the map ends where it ends for a kindness, and that the blank paper is not ignorance but a fence. Some of our steadiest neighbors believe going down there to look is exactly the sort of curiosity that gets a body added to the tally, and that the truest thing you can do for the town is to farm your acres, mind your creek, and let the question lie.

The Board does not know which of these is right. It is entirely possible that more than one is, or that none is, or that the honest answer is the dull one about moor and coast and no reason to go. But we notice that in a region that has mapped its coast and named its every trail and put a waystation on every northern mile, there is a whole direction we have agreed, without ever quite deciding, to leave off the paper. And we notice that this is the spring in which people have started to disappear.

We are not telling you to go south. If anything the Board is telling you, gently, not to. We are only saying that the question is out here now, spoken aloud at last, and that a thing spoken is at least a thing you can watch. If you know something true about what lies past the second creek, something you have seen with your own eyes and not inherited from a grandfather, the Board will hear it, by owl, and it will keep your name out of it if you ask. Until then the map ends where it ends, and we would all do well to stay this side of the blank.
