I should say at the outset that I am new to these pages, and that I know whose desk I am sitting at. Marigold Fenn covered wellness and openings for the Groves, and Marigold Fenn has not come home, and I did not take this beat lightly or take it with any illusions about the season we are in. So when I tell you the first opening I chose to write up is a good one, an honest one, the kind you can walk into without your shoulders climbing to your ears, please understand I chose it on purpose. We could all use a door that opens onto exactly what it says it does.

The door is in Dellriven, on the little high street below the manticore trail, and above it hangs a copper kettle gone soft green with age. The Green Kettle is a witch's apothecary, which in the Groves is an old and unglamorous trade, and its proprietor is a broad, floury-armed woman everyone already calls Mother Bettony, though the sign says Bettony Crane. She has been mixing remedies out of her home kitchen for thirty years. This week she finally has a shop.

I asked her, half out of habit and half because I have learned to ask, how it works. She looked at me as though I had asked how bread works. "You want to know what is in it," she said, and then she told me. All of it. The sleeping tonic is valerian and Groves hops and a spoon of Dorran honey when she can get it, which she cannot, so clover for now, and she said so on the label. The tincture for a cough is thyme and mallow and a charm she will teach you if you stand still long enough. The little bottles are labeled in her own hand, ingredient by ingredient, and where a thing is charmed rather than merely steeped she writes the charm's name too, because, she said, "a body has a right to know what it is drinking."

I have thought a great deal, this spring, about the difference between that sentence and the ones I have heard elsewhere in these hills. We have all heard about the other opening, the one out on the Misthollow fire road, where the woman behind the door would tell you what it did to you but never how. Mother Bettony is the opposite of that in every particular, and she knows it, and she is not shy about it. "I do not deal in doors," she told me, tart about it. "I deal in kettles. You can see the bottom of a kettle." I have not stopped turning that over since.

The shelves are the real pleasure. Tonics and tinctures and salves along one wall, yes, but the other wall is given over to trinkets, and this is where the shop stops being a pharmacy and starts being a place you want to linger. Charm-knots for a colicky baby. Iron nails blessed against the wrong sort of visitor, which several Dellriven customers were buying by the handful this week and which I will let you draw your own conclusions about. Hag-stones on cords. Little clay hearth-guardians, ugly as sin and apparently very effective. A bowl of what she calls "finding-pins," for things you have lost, though she admitted with a shrug that they work better on keys than on people, and the shop went quiet for a moment when she said it, and she had the grace to look sorry she had.

And then there is the back. Mother Bettony has taken the long room behind the shop, knocked out a wall, and is building a restaurant in it, an actual sit-down supper room she is calling The Long Table. It is not open yet. There is sawdust on the floor and a hearth half-laid and one enormous salvaged table down the center that gives the place its name. But you can see exactly what she means it to be. "The tonic is for when you are already sick," she said. "The table is so you do not get that way. Hot food, other people, a lamp on. That is half of medicine and nobody bottles it." She means to serve the plain restorative cooking of the Groves, the broths and the bread and the root things, at a price she keeps insisting will be "fair or nothing," and she means to keep a few seats always open for anyone who should not be eating alone that night. In this season, in these hills, I found I had to look at the half-built hearth for a moment before I could write that down.

I cover wellness for these pages now, and I want to be plain about why an apothecary and a half-finished eatery belong in my column rather than the trade notices. We have spent this whole strange spring being asked to trust things we are not allowed to understand. The Green Kettle is the antidote to that mood, not in a bottle but in its bones. It is a place built on the radical Groves proposition that you deserve to know what is in the cup and who is at the table. Mother Bettony would tell you that is not wellness, it is just decency, and that they used to be the same word.

Go by while the sawdust is still down, is my advice. Buy a hag-stone. Ask her what is in the thing before you drink it, and watch how pleased she is that you did. The Long Table opens later this season, and I will be there on the first night it does, in a seat near the fire, and I will tell you all of it, because at The Green Kettle you are allowed to be told.
