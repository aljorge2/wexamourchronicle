I want to start by apologizing, because I promised you something and I am not going to deliver it. Last time out, On Record was three days with Stilver Lugrathe, a man I expected to find dull and found, instead, unreasonably warm. The whole idea of this segment is that if you sit with a person long enough, past the version they walk in with, you eventually meet the actual human being underneath. That is the job. That is the only thing I know how to do. This week I sat with the actor Cassian Swiggity, and I have to report to you that I went looking for the human being underneath and I do not believe I found one. So there is no episode. There is only this.

If you do not follow the pictures, Cassian Swiggity is the star of the new superhero spectacle that half of Vaelthir spent the spring looking at posters for. Caped, jawed, the whole business. You have seen his face on the side of the transit house whether you wanted to or not. His people reached out to us, which should have been my first warning, because in six years of this beat the guests worth having never once reached out to me. I went anyway. I am, apparently, an optimist.

Here is how it started. Three days before I was to meet him, his office sent over what they called, without any embarrassment, a rider. I have interviewed the Chair of ORCM and I did not sign anything. For Cassian Swiggity I was sent four pages. I am going to tell you what was in them, because I think it is the whole man and I do not think I need to add a word.

He must win every fight. That is a contract clause. Not a preference, a clause, in writing, that in any picture he appears in his character must win every physical confrontation on screen. I asked, later, in person, what happens in a story that requires him to lose one. He looked at me as though I had proposed something obscene and said, "Then it is not my story."

He must be the romantic lead, and he must get the girl. Also a clause. In every picture he must play the love interest, he must be the one the audience is meant to want, and the romance must resolve in his favor. And the love interest must be a girl. That word is in the document. I want to be careful and precise here because he was: the clause specifies that his character may only ever be paired with a woman, full stop, and I asked him about it directly and he repeated it back to me slower, as if the slowness would help me, "It has to be a girl."

And the one that I keep coming back to, the one that I think tells you more about him than the other two combined: if the romance in the story has to end, he must be the one who ends it. He cannot, by contract, be left. If the script calls for a breakup, the writers are required to rework it so that Cassian's character does the breaking up. He is never dumped. He is never the one standing in the doorway watching someone else decide. I asked him why that mattered so much, in a made-up story, about a made-up man, and he said, and I wrote this down exactly, "Because people believe what they see. And they are never going to see me get left."

That was most of what I got, by the way. That exchange. Because the three days I was promised turned into a little under three hours, most of which I spent in a corridor. He kept me waiting the first afternoon and then decided he was tired. The second day his people moved the location twice and then told me he would only speak if I agreed in advance not to ask about the picture's opening. Which brings me to the part I am, professionally, obligated to include.

The picture is not doing well. I had been trying to be delicate about it out of some reflex of manners, but he made delicacy impossible by forbidding the subject, so I will simply report the fact: the new Cassian Swiggity superhero spectacle, the one on the side of the transit house, opened to empty houses and has stayed that way. The ticket halls are running it to a scatter of people in a room built for hundreds. By every account I could gather it has, in the plainest terms, tanked. And I want to be fair, box office is not character, a good person can be in a picture nobody sees. But I raise it because of the timing. I sat across from a man who has written it into law that he cannot lose a fight and cannot be left, in the same week that the whole region, given the free choice, quietly declined to show up for him. He has built an entire self out of never being rejected, and he is being rejected at a scale I am not sure he has the equipment to survive, and he spent our interview forbidding me to say the word.

That is the closest I got to the human being underneath. Not in anything he offered, because he offered nothing, but in that gap. The man with the no-losing clause, losing. And I think if I had gotten my three days, if he had let me stay, that is the story that would have been worth telling, the one about what happens to a person who has arranged his whole life so that the word "no" can never legally reach him, on the week it reaches him anyway. But he did not let me stay. He decided he was tired.

So there is no episode. I could not make one out of a man determined not to be met. What I can tell you is what I saw, which is a very frightened person in an expensive cape, and I can tell you the thing On Record exists to remind us of, which is that the people who most need you to see only the poster are usually the ones with the most going on behind it. I just could not get behind this one. He had it in writing that I never would.

*Joey Vaelwick is the Chronicle's long-form correspondent and the voice behind On Record on the Vaelthir Hour. He would like the record to reflect that he did, in fact, bring tea. Corrections, tips, and future guests who will actually sit down may be sent by owl.*
